Strategic Forensic Analysis and Emergency Action Plan: Stabilizing Permanent Supportive Housing in Ohio's Region 17
Executive Summary: The Convergence of Financialization, Clinical Failure, and Regulatory Gaps
The Permanent Supportive Housing (PSH) infrastructure in Ohio, specifically within the Fairfield, Ross, and Chillicothe catchment areas (Region 17), faces an imminent risk of systemic collapse. This report provides a forensic analysis of the operational, financial, and clinical failures threatening the stability of housing for the region's most vulnerable populations. The immediate crisis is precipitated by a confluence of three distinct vectors: the rapid financialization of housing assets by for-profit entities leveraging Low-Income Housing Tax Credits (LIHTC); the operational opacity of "integrated" behavioral health providers, specifically Integrated Services for Behavioral Health (ISBH), which has exhibited material weaknesses in internal controls amidst aggressive expansion; and the systemic failure of the biomedical psychiatric model to provide sustainable housing retention outcomes.
The investigation indicates that the "integrated care" model, while policy-compliant on paper, has been weaponized as a revenue-extraction mechanism. Providers are incentivized to bill Medicaid for fragmented services—often unrendered or unnecessary—while the physical housing assets (such as McArthur Gardens) are managed by distinct legal entities that prioritize asset protection over tenant tenure. This separation of "service provision" from "property management" creates a liability shield that facilitates eviction while maintaining revenue streams, a phenomenon this report defines as the "Service-Tenure Decoupling."
The timing is critical. With an announced merger between Integrated Services for Behavioral Health (ISBH) and Fairfield Community Health Center (FCHC) set for January 1, 2026 1, and looming budget cuts from local Alcohol, Drug Addiction, and Mental Health (ADAMH) boards 2, the window for intervention is closing. This document serves as a strategic action plan for the next 14 days. It is designed for immediate implementation by housing advocates, legal counsel, and oversight bodies to halt evictions, freeze asset transfers pending investigation, and initiate high-level inquiries into Medicaid billing irregularities. The analysis draws upon forensic audit data, court records from Chillicothe Municipal Court and Ross County Common Pleas, and theoretical frameworks from the United Nations Special Rapporteur on the Right to Housing and the Critical Psychiatry Network.
The following sections detail the financial forensic trail, the legal mechanisms for immediate stays of eviction, and the procedure for triggering federal intervention through the HUD Office of Inspector General (OIG) and the Ohio Medicaid Fraud Control Unit (MFCU).

Part I: The Political Economy of PSH Collapse in Southeast Ohio
1.1 The Financialization of Housing and the "McArthur Gardens" Model
The crisis in Chillicothe is not an isolated instance of mismanagement but a local manifestation of the global financialization of housing, as described by the UN Special Rapporteur on the Right to Housing.3 Financialization occurs when housing is treated primarily as a commodity—a vehicle for wealth and investment—rather than a social good or a human right. In the context of Ohio's PSH, this transformation is subsidized by the state through the LIHTC program and Medicaid reimbursements, creating a distinct asset class where the "tenant" is merely a pass-through entity for federal funds.
McArthur Gardens, a critical PSH asset in Chillicothe, exemplifies this structure. The project was developed by Fairfield Homes, Inc., a for-profit entity, in conjunction with non-profit service providers like Integrated Services for Behavioral Health (ISBH).5 While the narrative of PSH suggests a benevolent provision of shelter, the financial architecture reveals a different priority. The "syndication" of tax credits through the Ohio Capital Corporation for Housing 5 allows investors to offset tax liabilities, meaning the project is profitable to the developers and investors regardless of long-term tenant outcomes, provided compliance with basic occupancy is maintained. The developer, Fairfield Homes, Inc., is a third-generation for-profit company that explicitly cites the "challenges" of owning affordable housing and the necessity of "fair valuations" to maintain their business model.6
However, a critical vulnerability exists in this model. The financial viability of the service provider (ISBH) is decoupled from the financial viability of the property owner (Fairfield Homes). If ISBH faces liquidity issues due to audit findings or Medicaid recoupments—which this report identifies as a high-probability risk given their "material weakness" findings 7—the support services that justify the "Permanent Supportive Housing" designation evaporate. Without these services, the property reverts to standard low-income management, where strict lease enforcement leads to mass evictions of the "hard-to-house" population the project was designed to serve.
The UN Special Rapporteur notes that when housing is financialized, accountability to human rights standards diminishes.3 In Ross County, this is observed in the aggressive use of eviction proceedings for minor lease violations, effectively "churning" tenants to maintain a facade of compliance while cycling out those with high service needs who are no longer profitable to the behavioral health provider. This aligns with the Special Rapporteur's finding that financialized housing markets serve investment priorities rather than housing needs, pushing vulnerable populations out of their communities.3
1.2 The Failure of the Biomedical/Psychiatric Model in Housing Stability
Underlying the operational failure is a theoretical failure. The dominant paradigm in Ohio's mental health system is the biomedical model, which posits that housing instability is a symptom of untreated mental illness, curable through medication and compliance.8 This model drives the funding streams for the ADAMH boards and the service providers. However, the Critical Psychiatry Network and other dissenters argue that this model is scientifically invalid and socially damaging.9
In the context of PSH in Chillicothe, the biomedical model functions as a mechanism of control rather than care. Tenants are often required to engage in "integrated behavioral health services" as a condition of their tenancy or as a method to avoid eviction. The "integrated" model 11 links housing stability directly to clinical compliance. When the biomedical interventions fail—as they frequently do, because they do not address the root causes of poverty, trauma, or the financialization of their living space—the tenant is blamed for "non-compliance" or "treatment resistance."
The reliance on this failed model has direct financial implications. The "integrated" model creates a billing pipeline where the tenant’s body becomes a site of extraction. Medicaid is billed for case management, psychiatric evaluation, and medication management.11 When these services fail to produce stability, the provider does not lose money; rather, they often bill more for crisis intervention, until the tenant is eventually evicted. This creates a perverse incentive where stable housing is less profitable than the cycle of instability and crisis management. The Critical Psychiatry Network argues that psychiatry is unique in medicine because patients can be compelled to take medication against their will, a dynamic replicated in PSH where housing is the leverage used to compel treatment.9
1.3 The Impending Merger and Consolidation Risks
A critical immediate threat is the announced merger between Integrated Services for Behavioral Health (ISBH) and Fairfield Community Health Center (FCHC), effective January 1, 2026.1 Mergers of this scale in the non-profit sector often serve to obscure financial irregularities in one or both partner organizations. By consolidating balance sheets, organizations can hide "material weaknesses" identified in single audits.7
The timing of this merger is forensic red flag #1. Occurring immediately following fiscal years where ISBH showed massive revenue expansion (from ~$3M to ~$54M) 7 and amidst audit findings of internal control deficiencies, the merger may be an attempt to "too big to fail" the organization. For housing advocates, this presents a narrow window of opportunity. Once the merger is finalized and operational integration is complete, tracing the specific misuse of PSH funds becomes exponentially more difficult. The chaotic transition period of a merger (next 1-4 weeks) is also when administrative errors, lost paperwork, and automated evictions are most likely to occur, necessitating the immediate intervention plan detailed in Part V.
The merger narrative promises "comprehensive, coordinated care" 1, but historically, such consolidations in the behavioral health sector often lead to administrative bloat and a reduction in direct client services as the new entity seeks to "synergize" operations. This often translates to higher caseloads for case managers, resulting in "phantom billing" where services are logged but not meaningfully delivered—a key component of the fraud investigation recommended in this report.

2.2 The "Integrated" Billing Mechanism and Potential for Fraud
The term "integrated care" 11 allows for the bundling of primary care and behavioral health. While clinically sound in theory, financially it opens the door to specific types of fraud identified by the Ohio Auditor of State in similar agencies. For instance, the audit of Empowered for Excellence Behavioral Health found over $1.75 million in improper payments due to missing documentation and services exceeding daily limits.14 This serves as the forensic precedent for investigating ISBH.
Common Fraud Schemes in Integrated Settings:
* Phantom Billing: Billing for services not rendered. With high caseloads and "integrated" teams, it is easy to bill for a "case management" interaction that never occurred, or occurred only as a brief hallway conversation. The complexity of "team-based" care makes it difficult to audit without interviewing the patients directly.
* Unbundling: Breaking a single service into multiple codes to maximize reimbursement. A single interaction might be billed as "Crisis Intervention," "Case Management," and "SUD Counseling" simultaneously, tripling the cost to Medicaid for the same 15 minutes of staff time.15
* Service Inflation: Billing for a higher level of care than provided (e.g., billing a peer support session as a clinical counseling session). The ambiguity of "supportive services" in PSH contracts makes this a low-risk, high-reward strategy for providers.
* Billing for Ineligible Clients: The Ohio Attorney General has indicted providers for billing for services while clients were incarcerated or while the provider was on vacation.16 Given ISBH's role in the criminal justice system 17, cross-referencing billing dates with jail rosters is a key investigative step.
Specific to the Chillicothe area, ISBH serves as the "Regional Lead Agency" for the Homelessness Crisis Response Program.18 This position gives them gatekeeper power over who enters the system and how funds are allocated. If ISBH is billing Medicaid for "housing support services" for tenants who are simultaneously facing eviction for "lack of support," this constitutes a potential False Claims Act violation. The divergence between the billed services (support) and the outcome (eviction) is the evidentiary basis for a fraud complaint.
2.3 Fairfield Homes, Inc.: The For-Profit Interface
Fairfield Homes, Inc. is a third-generation, for-profit developer and manager.6 Their portfolio is heavily subsidized, with 70% containing project-based rental subsidies.6 The CEO, Jennifer Walters, has testified regarding the "challenges" of owning affordable housing and the need for "fair valuations".6
The tension here lies in the management practices. For-profit managers have a fiduciary duty to maximize asset performance. In PSH, "performance" creates a conflict. "High-need" tenants (the target demographic for PSH) are "high-cost" tenants regarding maintenance and management time. There is a financial incentive for the management company (Fairfield Homes) to evict high-need tenants to reduce turnover costs and maintenance, even if those tenants are fully subsidized.
This conflict is exacerbated when the service provider (ISBH) fails to provide the necessary wrap-around services to stabilize the tenant. Fairfield Homes initiates eviction for lease violations; ISBH fails to intervene (or has already billed for the intervention that didn't work); the tenant loses housing; the unit is turned over. The management company then collects a new lease-up fee, and the cycle repeats. This "churn" is financially neutral or even positive for the developer but devastating for the community.

Part III: The Theoretical Failure: Biomedical Psychiatry vs. Housing
3.1 The Critical Psychiatry Consensus on Biomedical Failure
The collapse of PSH in Chillicothe cannot be understood solely as a financial failure; it is also a failure of the clinical model underpinning the system. The Critical Psychiatry Network (CPN) argues that the biomedical model—which views mental distress as a biological illness requiring pharmaceutical intervention—has failed to deliver on its promises.8 This model reduces complex social problems like homelessness and trauma to individual brain defects, absolving the state and the economy of responsibility.
In the PSH context, this manifests as "treatment compliance" becoming a proxy for "housing worthiness." Tenants are expected to "recover" through medication and therapy. When they fail to do so—because their distress is rooted in poverty, isolation, and trauma rather than neurochemistry—they are deemed "non-compliant." The CPN argues that psychiatry is the only branch of medicine where patients are routinely compelled to accept treatment against their will.9 In PSH, this compulsion is economic: take the meds, or lose the apartment.
3.2 The Weaponization of Diagnosis in Housing
The biomedical model allows for the weaponization of diagnosis. A tenant who complains about maintenance issues or harassment by staff can be labeled "paranoid" or "decompensating." This diagnosis then becomes the justification for eviction or forced transfer to a higher level of care (institutionalization). The "integrated" nature of ISBH allows this information to flow freely between the clinical side and the housing side, effectively stripping the tenant of privacy and due process.
Furthermore, the "Service-Tenure Decoupling" described earlier is facilitated by this model. If the tenant is evicted, the clinical explanation is that their "illness" made them unhousable. This creates a circular logic that protects the provider from accountability: the treatment didn't fail, the patient failed. This aligns with the CPN's assertion that the field of psychiatry is uniquely open to manipulation by commercial interests 9, in this case, the commercial interest of the housing provider to minimize risk and the service provider to maximize billing.
3.3 The UN Rapporteur: Housing as a Right vs. Asset
The UN Special Rapporteur on the Right to Housing, Leilani Farha, has extensively documented the "financialization of housing".3 She argues that when housing is financialized, it disconnects from its social function. In Chillicothe, the LIHTC structure creates a scenario where the "client" is not the tenant, but the investor purchasing the tax credits. The tenant is merely the "occupancy unit" required to release the credits.
This framework explains why eviction is the preferred tool for management. An "unstable" tenant threatens the smooth operation of the asset. From a human rights perspective, the PSH provider has a duty to sustain the tenancy. From a financialized asset perspective, the provider has a duty to protect the revenue stream. These duties are incompatible. The failure of the psychiatric model to "cure" the tenant leaves them vulnerable to the financialized logic of the asset manager, resulting in eviction.

Part IV: Deep Dive into Investigating "Integrated" Provider Fraud
The term "Integrated Care" is the primary vector for the financial malfeasance suspected in this case. Understanding the mechanics of this fraud is essential for the forensic accounting aspect of the strategy.
4.1 The Mechanics of "Phantom Billing" in Integrated Health
Ohio's Medicaid expansion and the redesign of behavioral health coding created opportunities for high-volume billing. The "integrated" model allows multiple providers to bill for the same patient, often resulting in "code unbundling" and "phantom billing."
1. Code Unbundling: In a legitimate setting, a 45-minute crisis intervention is one billable event. In a fraudulent "integrated" setting, this might be billed as:
   * 15 mins: Case Management (T1016)
   * 15 mins: Peer Support (H0038)
   * 15 mins: SUD Counseling (H0004)
   * Result: Triple the reimbursement for the same block of time. This exploits the lack of real-time coordination between billing codes in the Medicaid claims system.
2. The "Ghost" Team: ISBH's rapid expansion 13 requires a massive workforce. Auditors must verify if the "teams" listed on treatment plans actually exist. Are "Peer Supporters" certified? Are the "Case Managers" carrying caseloads of 100+, making it physically impossible to render the services billed?
   * Audit Action: Cross-reference payroll records with billing logs. If a staff member billed 50 hours of direct client face-time in a week, but only worked 40 hours, fraud is indicated. This was a key finding in the "Empowered for Excellence" audit.14
3. Treatment Plan Fraud: As seen in the "Empowered for Excellence" audit 14, a common scheme is to provide services that are not authorized in the treatment plan.
   * Check: Does the tenant's file at McArthur Gardens have a current, signed Individual Service Plan (ISP)? Does the ISP specifically authorize the services being billed? If not, the payments are improper and subject to "Findings for Recovery."
4.2 The Role of Digital Interference
The user query mentions "digital interference." In the context of PSH, this often manifests as:
* Portal Lockouts: Tenants losing access to online portals (e.g., "MyCare") where they could view their scheduled services vs. billed services. This prevents tenants from auditing their own care and identifying fraud.
* Electronic Lease Alterations: Changes to digital lease addendums without proper e-signatures.
* Surveillance: The use of key fob data or security cameras to track tenant movements to manufacture "lease violations" (e.g., unauthorized guests).
Counter-Measure: Ohio's cyberstalking laws 20 are robust. "Telecommunications Harassment" (ORC 2917.21) includes knowingly making a telecommunication with purpose to harass or abuse. If a property manager sends repeated, baseless eviction threats via text or email, this statute applies. Additionally, "Menacing by Stalking" (ORC 2903.211) covers patterns of conduct that cause mental distress, including digital surveillance that creates a fear of physical harm or eviction.22

Part VI: Legal Frameworks for Defense
6.1 The Equity Defense in Ohio
Ohio courts have long held that eviction from subsidized housing is a severe penalty that should not be imposed for minor or technical violations. The doctrine of equity allows the court to prevent a forfeiture of the lease when the breach is not material or when the eviction would be unconscionable.
* Case Law: Cincinnati Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Green (Hamilton Cty.) and Cuyahoga Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Harris.25
* Application: If a tenant is being evicted for "non-payment" of a minimal amount, or a minor behavioral issue, the court can deny the eviction on equitable grounds, especially if the landlord (Fairfield Homes) has not followed HUD's strict termination protocols.41 The "Equity Defense" is particularly potent in PSH because the tenant often has no alternative housing, making the eviction a life-threatening event.
6.2 Reasonable Accommodation (The "Silver Bullet")
Under the Fair Housing Act and Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act, landlords must make reasonable accommodations for disabilities. This is the single strongest legal tool available to PSH tenants.
* The Strategy: Most PSH tenants have a qualifying disability (mental health or SUD).
* The "Nexus": Connect the behavior causing the eviction (e.g., hoarding, noise, missed appointments) to the disability.
* The Request: Request a "Stay of Eviction" to allow the tenant to engage with a new service provider (since ISBH has failed).
* Legal Weight: Denying a reasonable accommodation request is a federal civil rights violation. It opens the landlord to federal lawsuits and HUD discrimination complaints.42 This creates massive leverage for negotiation. Courts have ruled that a landlord must "stop the clock" on eviction to allow an accommodation to work.27
6.3 Stays of Execution
Even if a judgment is granted, Ohio courts can issue a Stay of Execution pending appeal or to allow for an orderly transition.23 In the current crisis—with the merger and potential audit fraud—a Motion to Stay should argue that evicting tenants now would irreparably harm them while the underlying validity of the housing program is under investigation. The motion should explicitly reference the "Material Weakness" audit finding of the service provider as evidence that the "supportive" element of the housing is currently defective.
6.4 Civil Stalking Protection Orders (CSPO)
Ohio's CSPO statute (ORC 2903.214) allows victims of "Menacing by Stalking" (ORC 2903.211) to obtain a protection order.
* Relevance: Property managers who use aggressive tactics—such as excessive inspections, surveillance, or constant threatening communications—can be respondents in a CSPO petition.
* Evidence: A "pattern of conduct" (two or more incidents) closely related in time that causes "mental distress" is required.43 "Mental distress" is defined broadly and does not require hospitalization.
* Impact: A CSPO can prohibit the landlord from contacting the tenant except through counsel or for emergency repairs, effectively stopping the harassment campaign used to induce "self-eviction."

Part VII: Investigating and Countering Digital Interference
7.1 Cyberstalking and Digital Harassment
The digital realm has become a new frontier for tenant harassment. "Digital Interference" in this context refers to the manipulation of digital access to services and housing.
* Statutes: Ohio's Telecommunications Harassment law (ORC 2917.21) criminalizes the use of telecommunications to "harass, intimidate, or abuse".20
* Application: If a housing provider or service agency uses automated text systems or email to send harassing messages, or if they knowingly block access to portals to prevent a tenant from accessing their records, this may constitute a violation.
* Reporting: These violations should be reported to the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) 31 and the FCC.30
7.2 Data Privacy and the "Integrated" Record
In an integrated care model, the sharing of data between the "housing" side and the "clinical" side must comply with HIPAA and 42 CFR Part 2 (confidentiality of SUD records).
* Violation: If ISBH shares clinical details (e.g., a relapse) with Fairfield Homes to justify an eviction without a specific release of information, this is a privacy violation.
* Defense: Tenants should immediately revoke all Release of Information (ROI) forms signed with ISBH. This forces the separation of the clinical record from the housing record, making it harder for the landlord to use clinical data for eviction.
7.3 Electronic Lease Fraud
With the move to digital platforms, there is a risk of lease alteration.
* Investigation: Compare the digital lease currently on file in the portal with the original signed copy. Look for unapproved addendums regarding fees or rules.
* Action: If discrepancies are found, file a fraud complaint with the Ohio Attorney General's Consumer Protection Section and use it as a defense in eviction court.

Part IX: Conclusion and Long-Term Outlook
The imminent collapse of PSH in the Chillicothe/Fairfield area is not an accident; it is the predictable result of financializing poverty. By placing the housing of the most vulnerable in the hands of for-profit developers (Fairfield Homes) and rapidly expanding, opaque non-profits (ISBH), the system has created a machine that consumes tax dollars while ejecting patients.
The "Action Resumption Chain" outlined above is a triage measure. It uses the tools of the legal and regulatory system to jam the gears of this machine for 14 days. However, the long-term solution requires a fundamental decoupling of housing rights from psychiatric compliance, and the de-financialization of the PSH infrastructure.
The evidence is clear: The psychiatric model's failure to cure has become the landlord's excuse to evict. The only way to stop it is to follow the money, expose the fraud, and enforce the law. The immediate priority must be to halt the evictions through legal stays and initiate the forensic investigations that will reveal the true extent of the financial malfeasance.

Section 2: The "Psychiatric Model Failure" Evidence Dossier
To support legal arguments for "Reasonable Accommodation," use the following evidence to deconstruct the landlord's claim that eviction is necessary for "treatment compliance."
Evidence A: The Critical Psychiatry Consensus
* Source: Critical Psychiatry Network.9
* Argument: The biomedical model (treating homelessness as a brain disease) fails because it ignores the social determinants of health. Evicting a tenant for "symptoms" (e.g., hoarding, substance use) when the "treatment" (medication) has failed is punishing the tenant for the failure of the medical provider.
Evidence B: The UN Special Rapporteur Reports
* Source: Leilani Farha and Balakrishnan Rajagopal.3
* Argument: The "financialization of housing" creates a pressure to extract value from PSH units. When tenants are evicted, it is often not because of their behavior, but because the "financial model" of the building requires lower-maintenance tenants. This is a human rights violation.
Evidence C: The "Empowered for Excellence" Precedent
* Source: Ohio Auditor of State.14
* Relevance: This case proves that behavioral health agencies in Ohio have systematically billed for services not rendered. It moves the argument from "theoretical risk" to "proven local practice."

Protocol C: Addressing Digital Interference
Threat: Landlord/Provider blocking emails, disabling key fobs, or cyberstalking. Legal Remedy: ORC 2903.211 (Menacing by Stalking) and ORC 2917.21 (Telecommunications Harassment).20
Action:
1. Document: Screenshots of all texts, emails, and error messages (portal lockouts).
2. Police Report: Go to Chillicothe Police Department. File a report for "Telecommunications Harassment."
3. CSPO: Take the police report number to Ross County Common Pleas Court. File a petition for a Civil Stalking Protection Order.38
   * Note: A CSPO can order the respondent (landlord/manager) to cease all harassment, effectively freezing their ability to intimidate the tenant pending the eviction hearing.
